Chapter 5

Product Costing Systems

  • Product (inventoriable) costs are the costs of making the product: direct materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead. They sit in inventory and become an expense (Cost of Goods Sold) only when the units are sold.
  • Period costs are non-manufacturing costs (selling and administrative). They are expensed in the period incurred, regardless of when goods are sold.
  • Manufacturing overhead is every indirect factory cost: indirect materials, indirect labor, factory utilities, factory insurance, and depreciation on the factory building and production equipment. Watch the classic trap: factory depreciation is a product cost (overhead), not a period expense.
  • Inventory by organization type: service firms generally hold only supplies; merchandisers hold merchandise (plus supplies); manufacturers hold raw materials, work-in-process, and finished goods (plus supplies). Only manufacturers convert inputs into outputs, so only they carry RM/WIP/FG.
Quick test: "Could this cost be traced into a unit on the factory floor?" If yes → product cost (DM, DL, or overhead). If it's about selling or running the office → period cost.
Predetermined Overhead Rate (POHR)
POHR = Estimated total overhead / Estimated total allocation base
Applied overhead = POHR × actual activity used
  • Why use a predetermined rate? So jobs can be costed promptly during the period (you don't wait until year-end to learn actual overhead) and so unit costs aren't distorted by seasonal or cyclical swings in activity. It is not a fraud-prevention tool, and actual overhead is still tracked at period end.
  • The allocation base (cost driver) should be what actually drives overhead — commonly direct labor hours or machine hours.
  • Apply the rate using the job's actual base usage.
  • Costs flow: Raw Materials → Work-in-Process → Finished Goods → Cost of Goods Sold. The job cost sheet accumulates DM, DL, and applied overhead for each job.
  • Know how to walk the schedule in both directions — you may be given the totals and asked to solve backward for an ending balance.
Cost-Flow Relationships
Direct materials used = Beg RM + Purchases − End RM
Total manufacturing cost = DM used + DL + Overhead applied
COGM = Beg WIP + Total mfg cost − End WIP
COGS = Beg FG + COGM − End FG
  • Over- / under-applied overhead: compare overhead applied to overhead actually incurred. Applied > actual = over-applied; applied < actual = under-applied.
  • Process costing is used for continuous, homogeneous production (vs. job costing for distinct jobs). Costs are averaged across all units passing through the process.
  • Equivalent units (EU) translate partially-complete units into whole-unit equivalents.
  • Weighted-average method: combines beginning inventory work with current-period work (it does not separate out the prior period's effort the way FIFO does).
Weighted-Average Process Costing
EU = Units completed + (Ending WIP units × % complete)
Cost per EU = (Beginning cost + Current-period cost) / EU
Cost of goods completed = Units completed × Cost per EU
Cost of ending WIP = Ending EU × Cost per EU
Tip: when "all costs are added uniformly," materials and conversion share one completion percentage, so you only need a single equivalent-unit computation.
CostAbsorption CostingVariable Costing
Direct materials & direct laborProduct costProduct cost
Variable manufacturing overheadProduct costProduct cost
Fixed manufacturing overheadProduct cost (in inventory)Period cost (expensed now)
Selling & administrativePeriod costPeriod cost
  • The only difference is the treatment of fixed manufacturing overhead: capitalized into inventory under absorption costing, expensed immediately under variable costing.
  • When inventory increases (produce > sell), absorption income is higher (fixed overhead is deferred in inventory). When inventory decreases, absorption income is lower. Equal production and sales → the two methods give the same income.

Chapter 6

Activity-Based Costing & Management

MethodHow overhead is applied
Plantwide rateOne rate for the whole plant, using a single base (e.g., total direct labor hours).
Departmental ratesA separate rate for each department, each using the base that best fits that department (e.g., DL hours in one department, machine hours in another).
Activity-based costingA separate rate for each activity cost pool, using the cost driver for that activity.
  • Plantwide: rate = total overhead / total base; apply by multiplying the rate by each product's base usage, then build product cost = DM + DL + applied overhead.
  • Departmental: rate for each department = that department's overhead / that department's base. Apply each department's rate using that department's driver. A job touches both departments, so add the two pieces.
LevelDriven by …
Unit-levelEach individual unit (direct materials, per-unit power, per-unit inspection).
Batch-levelEach batch/run, regardless of its size (machine setups, retooling between runs, a purchase order).
Product-levelAn entire product line (product design, branding, engineering a new model).
Facility-levelThe plant as a whole (plant insurance, property taxes, general-purpose equipment, the plant manager's salary).
How to classify: ask "what makes this cost go up?" One more unit → unit-level. One more batch → batch-level. One more product in the line → product-level. None of those — it just keeps the doors open → facility-level.
ABC Application
Activity rate = Activity cost pool / total cost-driver volume
Overhead applied to a product = Σ(Activity rate × driver units the product uses)
  • Stage 1: gather overhead into activity cost pools. Stage 2: assign each pool to products using its driver.
  • Cost distortion: compared with ABC, a traditional volume-based system overcosts high-volume, low-complexity products and undercosts low-volume, high-complexity products. ABC corrects this by tracing costs to the activities each product actually consumes.
  • ABC applies beyond the factory: assign customer-service / support activity costs (visits, calls, special handling) to each customer using the appropriate drivers.
  • Customer profit = the customer's gross profit − assigned support costs. Two customers with identical gross profit can have very different bottom-line profitability if one consumes far more support resources.
  • The customer profitability profile ("whale curve") plots cumulative profit against customers ranked most- to least-profitable. Cumulative profit rises to a peak, then declines — customers past the peak are unprofitable and erode total profit. The peak marks the number of profitable customers; the most expensive/least profitable customers are the ones to analyze and fix.
  • A multi-level (cost-hierarchy) contribution statement peels costs off in tiers — subtract unit-level costs to get a unit-level margin, then batch-, product-, customer-, and finally facility-level costs. This shows profitability at each level, not just the bottom line.
  • Break-even / target volume works the same as in CVP (Chapter 3): the contribution earned per unit (or per customer) must cover the higher-level costs plus any target profit. Solve required volume = (higher-level costs + target profit) / contribution per unit, and round customer counts up.
  • When the "contribution" is a margin on each sales dollar (e.g., a commission rate net of a per-dollar cost), divide the fixed/structural costs by that contribution rate to find the sales dollars needed to break even.
Connection to Exam 1: this is the CVP profit equation applied at the activity/customer level — revisit the Chapter 3 break-even and target-profit formulas if this feels rusty.

Chapter 7

Service Allocation, Capacity & Lean Operations

  • Service departments (maintenance, IT, payroll) support production departments. Their costs are allocated to production departments using a reasonable base.
  • Direct method: allocate each service department's cost only to production departments — ignore services provided to other service departments.
  • Step (step-down) method: allocate service departments in sequence, recognizing services provided to other service departments. Start with the department that serves the most other service departments.
  • Three capacity measures: theoretical (the absolute maximum, never achievable), practical (realistic sustainable output allowing for downtime), and normal/budgeted (expected actual usage).
  • Use practical capacity as the denominator for the overhead rate that goes on a customer quote. This charges products only for capacity they use and isolates the cost of idle capacity.
  • Denominator matters: the larger the capacity denominator, the lower the rate. Using theoretical capacity yields a rate that is too low — it understates unit product cost and leaves a large pool of unassigned cost.
Cost of Excess (Unused) Capacity
Capacity rate = Budgeted capacity cost / Practical capacity
Cost of excess capacity = (Practical capacity − capacity used) × rate
Who bears it? The cost of idle capacity should not be buried in product cost. It is reported separately as a period cost of unused capacity so management can see and manage it — charging it to the product would inflate unit cost and can trigger a "death spiral."
  • Just-in-time (JIT) is a pull system: produce in response to demand, minimizing inventory storage and waiting time. It is the opposite of a "just-in-case" / push (forecast-driven, buffer-stock) approach.
  • Cycle time (throughput time) is the total time to move a batch/unit through the process.
  • Manufacturing cycle efficiency (MCE) measures how much of that time is value-adding. Only processing time adds value; moving, waiting, and inspection do not. A higher MCE = a more efficient process.
Cycle Time & Efficiency
Cycle (throughput) time = processing + inspection + movement + waiting time
MCE = Value-added (processing) time / Total cycle time

Chapter 8

Pricing, Target Costing & Continuous Improvement

  • Cost-plus pricing builds a selling price by adding a markup on top of a cost base. The markup must cover all the costs not in the base, plus the desired profit.
  • Variable-cost markup method: the base is total variable cost, so the markup must cover total fixed costs and target profit.
Variable-Cost Markup Method
Markup % = (Total fixed costs + Target profit) / Total variable costs
Unit selling price = Unit variable cost × (1 + Markup %)
  • Critique of cost-based pricing: it ignores customer demand. Because the price is built from internal cost data alone, the resulting price may be one customers are unwilling to pay. (Cost data themselves are readily available — that is not the weakness.)
Target Costing
Target cost = Target (market) price − Required profit per unit
  • Target costing is market-driven and runs in reverse: start from the price the market will bear and the profit the company requires, then back into the cost the product must hit.
  • The product is then engineered (design for production) to meet that target cost — cost management happens up front, in design, not after the fact.
Kaizen / Continuous-Improvement Budget
Next-period budget = Current cost × (1 + growth rate) × (1 − improvement target %)
  • Kaizen costing builds a continuous cost-reduction target into the budget. When volume (and therefore cost) is also expected to grow, apply the growth factor and the improvement reduction — grow first, then trim by the improvement target.
  • Benchmarking is the systematic search for, and adoption of, the best practices used by leading organizations in order to improve performance.
  • Related continuous-improvement ideas: total quality management (TQM), the costs of quality, and lean/JIT (Chapter 7) all push toward eliminating waste and improving over time.
