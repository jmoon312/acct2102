Company Information

Harbor Goods Co. — budgeting for March

everything you need is here — and a few facts you won't

Harbor Goods Co. is a merchandiser that buys and resells one product — heavy-duty storage bins. The controller is assembling the company's master budget for the month of March: the operating schedules, the cash budget, and the budgeted income statement. Every fact she has is listed below. Pull the pieces you need for each step; not all of them belong in every schedule.

  • Selling price: $20 per unit. Purchase cost: $8 per unit.
  • For reference, the prior year's March generated $140,000 in sales revenue.

Collections

  • 30% of each month's sales are cash sales; the other 70% are on credit.
  • Credit sales are collected 20% in the month of sale, 50% the following month, and 25% two months after the sale.
  • The remaining 5% of credit sales is never collected (written off as uncollectible).

Inventory & purchases

  • Desired ending inventory each month = 25% of next month's unit sales.
  • Inventory on hand March 1: 2,000 units.
  • Actual February merchandise purchases: $52,000.
  • Suppliers are paid 40% in the month of purchase, 60% the following month.

Selling expenses (monthly)

  • Sales commissions: 5% of sales revenue.
  • Variable miscellaneous: 2% of sales revenue.
  • Fixed salaries $6,000; advertising $3,000; depreciation $4,000 (non-cash).
  • All selling expenses are paid in the month incurred.

General & administrative (monthly)

  • Salaries $10,000; insurance $1,500; utilities $1,200; depreciation $2,000 (non-cash); miscellaneous $800.
  • Cash G&A is paid 70% in the month incurred, 30% the following month.
  • February cash G&A totaled $12,500.

Taxes & financing

  • Income tax = 30% of pretax income, paid the month after it is incurred.
  • Income taxes payable on March 1: $12,000.
  • Note payable $40,000 at 6% annual interest — $200 of interest accrues each month.
  • Minimum required ending cash balance: $25,000. Cash on hand March 1: $22,000.
  • A $50,000 equipment purchase (cash) is planned for March.
  • The company employs 18 people and has $22,000 of common stock outstanding.

Budgeted income statement — March

where cash and profit finally part ways

The final piece. The income statement uses accrual figures, not cash — so depreciation is back in, uncollectible accounts show up as an expense, and timing of payments no longer matters. You already have March sales revenue from Step 1, and the monthly interest accrual on the note is your interest expense.
