Step 2

Screen each opportunity

evaluate them in isolation first

Two proposals land on the CFO's desk the same week:

Proposal A — ProConnect Plus: HP's engineering team has developed an upgraded adapter with USB-C Power Delivery. The upgrade adds $5 per unit in processing cost and sells for $29 instead of $20. Each Plus unit requires 1.5 standard-equivalent units of production capacity (extra testing and calibration). HP commissioned a market study for $8,000 last quarter that confirmed demand for the upgrade.

Proposal B — University order: A state university system wants 1,500 standard adapters at $14 per unit as a one-time bulk purchase. The university handles its own distribution, so HP incurs no variable S&A. The university requires the full 1,500 units — no partial shipments.

Evaluate each proposal on its own merits — ignore capacity for now.

Step 3

Allocate scarce capacity

both proposals can't fully coexist — which wins?

HP has only 1,000 spare capacity units. Each Plus upgrade consumes 0.5 extra capacity units (1.5 total vs. 1.0 standard). The university order needs 1,500 capacity units but only 1,000 are available — accepting the full order means turning away 500 regular customers who pay $20 each.

HP must decide: upgrade adapters to Plus, accept the university order, or do nothing.
