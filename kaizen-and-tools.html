Year 2 budget — all three categories

same growth, different Kaizen targets per category

The three categories share the same 6% volume growth but face different Kaizen reduction targets. Labor and supervision are squeezed hardest (3%) because they're under direct management control. Step 4

Which tool is at work?

target costing · Kaizen · benchmarking · cost-based pricing

The chapter introduced four ways managers think about cost and price. Each of the scenarios below leans on one of them. Read the scenario; pick the tool.

Quick reminders: cost-based pricing starts from cost and adds markup. Target costing starts from a market price and works backward to a cost ceiling, usually during product design. Kaizen costing sets year-over-year cost-reduction targets on existing operations. Benchmarking studies an outside reference point to set internal goals.

Match each scenario to the right tool.

A consumer electronics company designs a new smart speaker backward from a $79 retail price point. Engineering, suppliers, and contract manufacturers are all given a $31 per-unit cost ceiling they must hit before the product gets greenlit.
A custom-cabinetry shop bids a kitchen install by adding up estimated direct materials and labor, then layering on a 35% markup to cover overhead and profit.
An auto-parts plant runs weekly continuous-improvement huddles to find 1% cost takeouts on the existing assembly line — trimming setup time, eliminating scrap, reorganizing tools at workstations.
A product-design team is told a new fold-flat tote must cost no more than $4.10 per unit to manufacture so it can sell at the established $9.99 retail price and clear the brand's margin requirement.
A hospital system subscribes to a peer-reporting service that compares its cost per discharge against 40 similar hospitals nationwide. The CFO uses the median figures as targets for next year's budget planning.
A warehouse supervisor announces a 2% monthly cycle-time reduction target on existing inbound-receiving operations, and reviews variance reports with the team every Friday.
A small specialty printer prices its custom job quotes by computing full manufacturing cost per job and applying a 40% markup, with no formal check against what competitors charge.
Crescent's operations director visits two best-in-class fulfillment centers in Ohio to study how they organize pick paths, then sets new internal productivity goals based on what she observed.
Step 5

Build your own — stress-test the budget

three planning scenarios for Crescent's CFO

Three planning what-ifs. Each one varies the baseline (P&P $60,000, Pkg $15,000, Ship $45,000, growth 6%, Kaizen targets 3% / 2% / 1%) along one axis. Use Kaizen logic for each category.

Solve each one.

(a) A surprise viral product launch pushes expected volume growth to 10% across all three categories. Kaizen targets unchanged. What is the new Year 2 total budget?
(b) Crescent's COO pushes leadership to double every Kaizen target (P&P 6%, Pkg 4%, Ship 2%). Growth stays at 6%. What is the new Year 2 total budget?
(c) The CFO asks the reverse question: assuming 6% volume growth in every category, what single uniform Kaizen rate applied to all three categories would keep the total Year 2 budget exactly flat at $120,000?
