Chapter 1

Managerial Accounting: An Overview

  • Financial accounting communicates information to external users (shareholders, creditors). It is historical, aggregate, and must follow GAAP.
  • Managerial accounting communicates information to internal users (managers). It is future-oriented, created as needed for decision-making, and has no required standards.
  • Financial accounting produces periodic reports about assets, obligations, and performance. Managerial accounting produces special-purpose information and reports.
Think about it: Financial accounting answers "How did we do?" Managerial accounting answers "What should we do next?"
  • Mission — the broad purpose of the organization.
  • Goals — specific, measurable targets that support the mission.
  • Strategy — the plan for achieving goals. Three strategic positions:
    • Cost leadership — lowest cost in the industry (e.g., Wal-Mart)
    • Product/service differentiation — unique features that justify a premium (e.g., BMW)
    • Market niche — focused segment with tailored offerings (e.g., a regional airline combining leisure + cargo)
  • Performance measures should align with the company's chosen strategy.
  • The value chain is the set of value-producing activities from sourcing raw materials through delivery to the final consumer.
  • Exploiting the value chain means finding opportunities to reduce cost, improve quality, or enhance service across all linked activities — not just internal operations.
  • Value-added perspective focuses only on internal processes. The value chain perspective extends upstream (suppliers) and downstream (customers) for a fuller picture.
  • Organizations may keep activities in-house to maintain a core competency, control quality, or assure supply — even when an outside provider could do it cheaper.
  • Key dimensions of competition: quality, price (initial + ongoing operating cost), and service/support.
  • Growing use of data analytics, process improvement, and strategic thinking in the managerial accountant's role.
Chapter 2

Cost Behavior, Activity Analysis & Cost Estimation

  • Variable costs — change in total proportionally with activity (constant per unit). Example: direct materials.
  • Fixed costs — stay constant in total within the relevant range (decrease per unit as volume rises). Example: rent.
  • Mixed costs — contain both a fixed and a variable component. Equation: Y = a + bX.
  • Step costs — fixed within a narrow range, then jump to a new level (e.g., adding a supervisor for every 500 units of capacity).
  • The relevant range is the span of activity over which cost behavior assumptions hold. Outside this range, per-unit variable costs or total fixed costs may shift.
  • Committed fixed costs result from long-term decisions that cannot be easily changed. Examples: depreciation on a factory, a multi-year contract salary, long-term lease payments.
  • Discretionary fixed costs result from annual or short-term spending decisions that management can adjust. Examples: advertising, charitable donations, employee travel for development seminars, cancellable service contracts.
  • The key question: Can management easily change or eliminate this cost in the short run? If yes, discretionary. If it's locked in by a prior commitment, committed.
Total Cost Equation
Y = a + bX
where a = total fixed costs, b = variable cost per unit, X = activity level
  • Average cost per unit = Total cost / Units = (a/X) + b. As volume increases, the fixed-cost-per-unit piece (a/X) shrinks, so average cost decreases.
  • To find the volume where average cost equals a target: set (a/X) + b = target and solve for X.
MethodHow It WorksStrengths / Limits
High-Low Uses only the highest and lowest activity-level observations to compute b, then solves for a. Simple and fast. Uses only 2 data points — sensitive to outliers and ignores the rest of the data.
Scatter Diagram Plot all observations; visually fit a line. Helps spot outliers and non-linearity. Good diagnostic tool. Subjective — different people draw different lines.
Least-Squares Regression Statistically fits a line by minimizing the sum of squared residuals. Uses all data points. Objective, uses all data, provides R² (goodness of fit). Sensitive to outliers; results only valid within the relevant range.
High-Low Method
b = (Costhigh − Costlow) / (Unitshigh − Unitslow)
a = Total cost at either point − (b × Units at that point)
  • The cost model is a tool, not a decision — always sanity-check the output.
  • Linearity is an assumption. Real costs may be curvilinear, especially outside the relevant range.
  • Changes in production technology make historical cost data less reliable for predicting future costs. New technology requires re-identification of the appropriate cost driver.
  • When prices of raw materials or labor are rapidly increasing, cost estimations based on prior periods will underestimate future costs.
  • The shorter the time period, the harder it is to appropriately match activity and cost (timing mismatches between when costs are incurred and when activity occurs).
  • Direct costs are traceable to a specific cost object. Indirect costs are shared across multiple cost objects and must be allocated.
  • Manufacturing cost components: direct materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead (indirect).
  • The direct/indirect classification depends on the cost object. A cost that is direct to a division may be indirect to a specific product within that division.
  • Variable/fixed and direct/indirect are independent dimensions. A cost can be any combination: variable-direct (hourly production wages for that store), variable-indirect (a percentage-based fee allocated across products), fixed-direct (a store manager's salary for that specific store), or fixed-indirect (corporate HQ insurance).

Chapter 3

Cost-Volume-Profit Analysis

Profit Equation
Profit = (Price × Q) − (Variable Cost per Unit × Q) − Fixed Costs
  • This is the foundation of CVP analysis. Given any four of the five variables (profit, price, VC/unit, Q, fixed costs), you can solve for the fifth.
  • To build this equation, you need to separate mixed costs into their fixed and variable components (using the methods from Chapter 2).
  • The functional (GAAP) income statement groups costs by function: Cost of Goods Sold, then Selling & Administrative. Used for external reporting.
  • The contribution income statement groups costs by behavior: all variable costs first, then all fixed costs. Used for internal CVP analysis.
Contribution format:
Sales
− Variable costs (manufacturing + selling & admin)
= Contribution Margin
− Fixed costs (manufacturing + selling & admin)
= Net Income
  • Contribution margin (CM) = Sales − Variable Costs. It represents the amount available to cover fixed costs and generate profit.
  • CM per unit = Price − Variable Cost per Unit.
  • CM ratio = CM / Sales = CM per unit / Price. Useful for dollar-based analysis: if sales increase by $1, profit increases by the CM ratio.
  • Converting from functional to contribution format: You must know what percentage of each functional line (COGS, S&A) is variable vs. fixed, then regroup accordingly.
Break-Even & Target Profit
Break-even units = Fixed Costs / CM per unit
Target profit units = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) / CM per unit
  • At break-even, total revenue = total costs and profit = 0.
  • Sensitivity analysis: flex one variable at a time (price, VC, FC, or volume) and re-solve. If volume increases by N units, profit increases by N × CM per unit.
  • All costs can be accurately classified as either fixed or variable.
  • Cost behavior is linear within the relevant range (constant VC per unit, constant total FC).
  • Revenue behavior is linear (constant selling price per unit).
  • Analysis is for a single product or a constant sales mix.
What does a change in variable costs do? A reduction in variable costs increases the CM per unit, which changes the slope of the total cost line and reduces the break-even point — but only if fixed costs remain constant. If fixed costs increase by an offsetting amount, break-even may not change.
Weighted-Average Contribution Margin
WACM = Σ(CM per uniti × Sales mix %i)
Break-even units = Fixed Costs / WACM
Then split total units by the sales mix percentages.
  • A shift in the sales mix toward the higher-CM product lowers the break-even point; a shift toward the lower-CM product raises it.
  • Sales mix analysis is important in any multiple-product or service organization. A basic CVP assumption is that the sales mix is constant; in practice, mix shifts can have a significant impact on profitability.
  • You can also use a dollar-based approach: compute the weighted-average CM ratio and divide fixed costs by it to get break-even in dollars.
Degree of Operating Leverage
DOL = Contribution Margin / Net Income
  • Operating leverage measures how sensitive profit is to changes in sales volume.
  • A firm with high fixed costs (high leverage) sees profits amplified in both directions — larger gains when sales rise, larger losses when sales fall.
  • Use DOL as a multiplier: if DOL = 3 and sales increase by 10%, profit increases by approximately 30%.
Target Profit with Taxes
Required pre-tax profit = Target after-tax profit / (1 − tax rate)
Target units = (Fixed Costs + Required pre-tax profit) / CM per unit
  • Taxes don't change the break-even point (no tax when profit = 0).
  • For target after-tax profit: gross up the target to a pre-tax amount, then use the standard target-profit formula.

Chapter 4

Relevant Costs & Differential Analysis

  • Relevant costs/revenues are future amounts that differ between alternatives.
  • Sunk costs are costs already incurred — they are never relevant regardless of the decision.
  • Opportunity costs (the benefit forgone by not choosing the next-best alternative) are always relevant.
  • Outlay costs are relevant when they differ between alternatives.
  • Fixed costs are not automatically irrelevant. If a fixed cost can be avoided by choosing one alternative, it is relevant. Variable costs may be irrelevant if they are the same under both alternatives (e.g., same activity level).
  • Differential analysis compares only the differences between alternatives — costs and revenues that are the same under both options can be ignored.
  • Always consider qualitative factors (employee morale, customer relationships, brand reputation, supply reliability) alongside the numbers.
DecisionKey Comparison
Profit plan changes / Discontinue a segment Lost contribution margin vs. avoidable fixed costs. Keep the segment if CM exceeds avoidable FC.
Special order Incremental revenue vs. incremental costs. Accept if the order's revenue covers its variable costs (and any additional fixed costs) and excess capacity exists. At full capacity, include the opportunity cost of displaced regular sales.
Outsourcing (make or buy) Avoidable production costs vs. purchase price + opportunity costs. Outsource if buying is cheaper after accounting for any freed-up capacity value (e.g., renting out space).
Sell or process further Additional revenue from further processing vs. additional processing cost. Joint costs incurred before the split-off point are sunk and irrelevant.
Constrained resource Maximize CM per unit of the scarce resource, not CM per unit of product.
  • Compare the special order price to the incremental cost per unit (usually variable manufacturing costs, possibly with adjustments for savings on selling expenses, etc.).
  • Fixed costs are typically irrelevant if excess capacity exists (they don't change).
  • If the order requires using capacity currently serving regular customers, include the opportunity cost of lost regular-price sales.
  • For long-term special orders, use full cost (including allocated fixed costs) as a pricing floor, and consider cost escalation clauses.
  • List all costs currently incurred to make the product. Identify which are avoidable (would disappear if you stop making it).
  • Compare avoidable costs to the outside purchase price.
  • Add any opportunity cost of freed capacity (e.g., rent out the space, use it for another product).
  • Organizations sometimes keep production in-house even at higher cost to maintain a core competency, assure supply, or control quality.
  • Joint costs are costs incurred before the split-off point when producing multiple products simultaneously (e.g., crude oil refining, cattle processing, sawmill operations).
  • Joint costs are sunk for the sell-or-process-further decision — they've already been incurred regardless of what you do after the split-off.
  • Process further only if incremental revenue > incremental processing cost.
  • Identify the bottleneck — the constrained resource that limits overall throughput.
  • Maximize throughput at the bottleneck by scheduling the highest-CM-per-constraint-unit products first.
  • Management should not encourage full utilization of non-bottleneck resources (this just builds excess inventory without increasing output).
  • Short-term: schedule around the constraint. Long-term: invest to expand the bottleneck. Eliminating one bottleneck creates the next one.
