Problem 1 · Chapter 5

Manufacturing Cost Flows & Departmental Overhead

Summit Forge Manufacturing — two production departments. Overhead is applied with departmental predetermined rates: machine hours in Machining, direct labor hours in Finishing.
Budgeted: Machining — OH $1,800,000, DLH 25,000, MH 120,000.  Finishing — OH $960,000, DLH 80,000, MH 12,000.

(a) Predetermined rate per department  ·  (b) Total overhead on Job #512 (Machining 10 DLH / 90 MH; Finishing 150 DLH / 8 MH)  ·  (c) RM used, COGM, COGS ($ thousands)

(a) Departmental overhead rates

Each department divides its own budgeted overhead by the base named for that department — Machining by machine hours, Finishing by direct labor hours.

Machining: $1,800,000 ÷ 120,000 MH = $15.00 / MH

Finishing: $960,000 ÷ 80,000 DLH = $12.00 / DLH

TrapDon't divide Machining's overhead by its labor hours (or Finishing's by its machine hours). The base is fixed by the department, not chosen to match the bigger number.

(b) Overhead applied to Job #512

Apply each department's rate to the hours of its own base that the job used.

Machining: 90 MH × $15.00 = $1,350

Finishing: 150 DLH × $12.00 = $1,800

Total overhead applied = $1,350 + $1,800 = $3,150
TrapThe job's 10 Machining DLH and 8 Finishing MH are distractors — each department ignores the base it doesn't use. Four numbers are given; only two belong in the calculation.

(c) Cost-flow schedule ($ thousands)

(i) Raw materials used = Beg RM + Purchases − End RM

150 + 320 − 170 = $300

(ii) Cost of goods manufactured = RM used + DL + MOH + Beg WIP − End WIP

300 + 240 + 520 + 410 − 70 = $1,400

(iii) Cost of goods sold = Beg FG + COGM − End FG

280 + 1,400 − 200 = $1,480

RM used $300  ·  COGM $1,400  ·  COGS $1,480 (thousands)
TrapPut RM used (not RM purchased) into the WIP schedule, and remember to subtract the ending balance in each of the three stages. Each line is "beginning + additions − ending."
Problem 2 · Chapter 6

Activity-Based Costing & Customer Profitability

Part A — Cobalt Instruments makes Standard and Deluxe; overhead split into four pools: Materials handling $24,000 (requisitions), Machine setups $60,000 (setups), Machining $180,000 (machine hours), Inspection $36,000 (inspections).
Driver use — Standard: 30 req / 50 setups / 5,000 MH / 40 insp.  Deluxe: 20 req / 70 setups / 7,000 MH / 60 insp.

Part B — three customers, each $600,000 gross profit before support. Support rates: orders $300, rush orders $150, visits $500. Use — Apex 400/100/60, Borealis 900/350/120, Crown 500/120/70.

Part A: (a) activity rate per pool, (b) overhead assigned to each product.  Part B: (a) profit per customer after support, (b) which to analyze further.

Part A (a) — Activity rates

Rate = pool cost ÷ total driver units (Standard + Deluxe).

Materials handling: $24,000 ÷ 50 = $480 / requisition

Machine setups: $60,000 ÷ 120 = $500 / setup

Machining: $180,000 ÷ 12,000 = $15 / MH

Inspection: $36,000 ÷ 100 = $360 / inspection

Part A (b) — Overhead assigned

Standard: 30×480 + 50×500 + 5,000×15 + 40×360 = 14,400 + 25,000 + 75,000 + 14,400 = $128,800

Deluxe: 20×480 + 70×500 + 7,000×15 + 60×360 = 9,600 + 35,000 + 105,000 + 21,600 = $171,200

Standard $128,800  ·  Deluxe $171,200
Check: 128,800 + 171,200 = $300,000 = sum of the four pools. All overhead is assigned. ✓

Part B (a) — Customer profitability after support

Support cost = $300×orders + $150×rush + $500×visits; then profit = $600,000 − support.

CustomerSupport costProfit after support
Apex120,000 + 15,000 + 30,000 = $165,000$435,000
Borealis270,000 + 52,500 + 60,000 = $382,500$217,500
Crown150,000 + 18,000 + 35,000 = $203,000$397,000

Part B (b) — Which to analyze

Borealis. Same $600,000 gross profit as the others, but it consumes by far the most support resources (most orders, rush shipments, and visits), cutting its after-support profit to roughly half. It's where renegotiating order patterns, minimum order sizes, or rush-shipment terms would move profitability most.
TrapGross profit alone makes all three look identical. The whole point of ABC customer profitability is that cost-to-serve differs — rank by profit after support, not by gross profit. Don't spread support cost evenly across customers.
Problem 3 · Ch 7–8

Capacity, Cycle Efficiency & Pricing

A five-part rapid-fire across Chapters 7–8: (a) idle-capacity cost, (b) cycle time & efficiency, (c) cost-plus price, (d) target cost, (e) Kaizen budget. Givens are restated inside each part below.

(a) Cost of excess (idle) capacity

Cascade Outdoor: machine OH $360,000, practical capacity 7,200 MH, will run at 35%.

Rate = $360,000 ÷ 7,200 = $50 / MH

Used = 7,200 × 35% = 2,520 MH  →  Idle = 7,200 − 2,520 = 4,680 MH

(i) Cost of excess capacity = 4,680 × $50 = $234,000

(ii) No. The product line should absorb only the capacity it uses (2,520 × $50 = $126,000). The $234,000 of idle cost was caused by the capacity/seasonal-demand decision, not by the units made, so it is reported separately as a period cost of unused capacity — keeping unit cost stable and making idle capacity visible to management.

TrapThe rate uses practical capacity (7,200) as the denominator, not actual hours. Dividing by actual hours would bury the idle cost back in the product.

(b) Cycle time & manufacturing cycle efficiency

Cycle time = sum of all five stages. MCE = processing ÷ cycle time (only processing adds value).

SystemCycle timeMCE
System A180+60+40+80+20 = 380 min180 ÷ 380 = 47.4%
System B150+100+50+100+20 = 420 min150 ÷ 420 = 35.7%
(iii) System A is more efficient — higher MCE (47.4% vs 35.7%) and a shorter total cycle time (380 vs 420 min).
TrapOnly processing is value-added. Setup, movement, waiting, and inspection are all non-value-added — don't include them in the MCE numerator.

(c) Cost-plus pricing (variable-cost markup)

Riverbend: 50,000 units; variable $2,800,000 mfg + $1,200,000 S&A; fixed $3,500,000 mfg + $1,500,000 S&A; target profit $1,000,000.

Total variable = 2,800,000 + 1,200,000 = $4,000,000   Total fixed = 3,500,000 + 1,500,000 = $5,000,000

(i) Markup % = (fixed + target profit) ÷ total variable = (5,000,000 + 1,000,000) ÷ 4,000,000 = 150%

(ii) Price: unit variable = 4,000,000 ÷ 50,000 = $80  →  $80 × (1 + 1.50) = $200

Markup 150%  ·  Selling price $200 / unit
Check: 50,000 × $200 = $10,000,000 revenue − $9,000,000 total cost = $1,000,000 target profit. ✓
TrapWith a variable-cost base, fixed costs are not in the base — they go in the markup numerator along with profit. Putting fixed cost in the base double-counts it.

(d) Target costing

Crestline: cuts price to $780, requires $120 profit per unit.

Target cost = target price − required profit = $780 − $120 = $660

Target cost = $660 / unit
TrapBuild from the new $780 price. The old $900 price and the market-share story are context — they don't enter the calculation.

(e) Kaizen costing

Bakery-cafe: packaging $200,000 now, +6% with volume, 4% Kaizen reduction target.

Budget = current × (1 + growth) × (1 − improvement) = $200,000 × 1.06 × 0.96 = $203,520

Cost first rises with volume to $212,000, then the 4% target trims it to $203,520.

Year-2 packaging budget = $203,520
TrapApply the 4% reduction to the grown $212,000, not to the original $200,000. Two adjustments, in sequence.
Problem 4 · Chapter 5

Process Costing — Weighted Average

Part A — Birchwood Company, all costs added uniformly. Beginning WIP 6,000 units, 30% complete: DM $5,000, DL $7,000, OH $3,000. Added in March: DM $48,000, DL $65,000, OH $32,000. Completed 38,000 units; ending WIP 4,000 units, 50% complete.

Part B — Beginning 10,000 units (20% complete), ending 14,000 units (40% complete), 50,000 started. Equivalent units of conversion, weighted-average.

Part A: (i) cost of goods completed, (ii) cost of ending inventory.  Part B: equivalent units of conversion (multiple choice).

Part A — Cost completed & ending inventory

All costs uniform → one completion % and one blended cost per equivalent unit. Weighted average ignores the beginning-WIP % entirely.

Equivalent units = completed + (ending × %) = 38,000 + (4,000 × 50%) = 40,000 EU

Total cost to account for = beginning WIP + costs added = (5,000+7,000+3,000) + (48,000+65,000+32,000) = 15,000 + 145,000 = $160,000

Cost per EU = 160,000 ÷ 40,000 = $4.00

(i) Cost of goods completed = 38,000 × $4.00 = $152,000

(ii) Cost of ending inventory = 2,000 EU × $4.00 = $8,000

Completed $152,000  ·  Ending inventory $8,000
Check: 152,000 + 8,000 = $160,000 = total cost to account for. ✓
TrapThe beginning-WIP 30% is a distractor. Weighted average blends prior-period and current cost together, so the beginning completion % never enters the math.

Part B — Equivalent units of conversion

Units completed = beginning + started − ending = 10,000 + 50,000 − 14,000 = 46,000

Weighted-average EU = completed + (ending × %) = 46,000 + (14,000 × 40%) = 51,600

Answer: (c) 51,600 units
Why the other choices are wrong (a) 46,000 ignores the work done on ending WIP.   (b) 49,600 is the FIFO answer — it subtracts the 2,000 EU of prior-period work in beginning inventory (10,000 × 20%), which weighted average does not do.   (d) 60,000 wrongly treats ending WIP as 100% complete. The tell that it's weighted average: the beginning completion % is never used.
