Step 1

Why budget — and what does risk have to do with it?

true or false

Before the mechanics, the purpose. A budget is a company's financial plan for a future period — but planning is only half the story. Every plan sits on top of risk: the danger that things won't go the way the plan assumes. Read each statement and decide whether it's true or false.

Mark each statement True or False.

A budget helps move a company from a reactive style of management toward a proactive one.
Budgets serve only to plan ahead; they are not used as a basis for evaluating performance once the period is over.
Risk refers only to events with a negative impact — a fire, a cyberattack, a supply-chain disruption.
Enterprise risk management is the process of identifying, evaluating, and planning responses to risks.
Risk management means eliminating every risk before a budget can be approved.
A company can use its budget model to estimate the financial impact of a risk and to compare possible responses.
Step 2

Spot the approach in a company's process

read how each company budgets — name the method

Five fictional companies, five budget lines. None of them announces which method it's using — you have to read how the budget is actually built and recognize the approach behind it. The four approaches: output/input, activity-based, incremental, and zero-based.

Read each company's process and identify the budgeting approach it illustrates.

Riverstone Mills plans to ship 12,000 dress shirts next quarter. Its planner starts from that target, figures that each shirt consumes a set amount of cotton, and budgets cotton purchases to cover exactly what those shirts will require.
Beacon Diagnostics budgets its lab-equipment calibration cost by estimating how many calibration events it will run next year — about 240 — and pricing each one at the standard cost of a single calibration.
At Greythorn County's public library, the director sets next year's operating budget by taking the current year's appropriation and adding 3% for expected cost increases. Individual programs aren't re-examined.
When new leadership took over Halcyon Nonprofit, they required every department to start its budget request at zero and justify each program on its own merits, ranking them as competing funding packages.
Sundial Brewing forecasts it will brew 40,000 cases next year. It works back from that volume to budget the malt, hops, and packaging that those cases will require.
Step 3

Which approach should they use?

recommend the best-suited method

Now go past identifying to judging. In each case the organization hasn't committed to a method yet — you're told only about its situation: whether spending links cleanly to output, whether it has reliable history to lean on, and what its leaders actually care about. Recommend the approach that fits best.

For each situation, choose the most appropriate approach.

A two-year-old startup is budgeting for the first time. It has almost no reliable prior-year history and its monthly demand swings wildly — but it can tie each unit it plans to build to a known quantity of materials. Which approach should it use for its materials budget (and why is "last year plus a percent" a poor fit)?
After a decade of "last year plus a few percent," a federal agency's overhead has quietly ballooned. A new inspector general wants every program forced to re-justify its funding from the ground up. Which approach best serves that mandate?
A municipal water authority budgets its back-office costs — admin salaries, office supplies, utilities — where there's no measurable link between dollars spent and gallons delivered, and where prior years have been stable and uncontroversial. Which approach is the most practical fit?
A contract manufacturer's overhead is driven almost entirely by setups, inspections, and material-handling moves. Management wants the budget to reflect how many times each of those activities will actually be performed. Which approach fits best?
A furniture maker can precisely link the board-feet of lumber in each table to its production plan, and wants its lumber budget to scale directly with the number of tables it schedules. Which approach fits best?
